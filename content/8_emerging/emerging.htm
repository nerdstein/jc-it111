Emerging Technologies Analysis

Emerging technologies are innovations that beget new technologies, in other words, technologies that are completely new and possess the ability to transform our understanding of a particular field of study.  Examples abound in the field of medicine, computing, transportation, manufacturing, and energy.  How do we encourage the development of these innovations? And, what do we do to enhance their diffusion and market their potential?  Answers to these questions can increase company profits and even positively impact a nation’s GNP, not to mention cure diseases and help end hunger.  While these promises may seem exaggerated, without innovations, decline and disease will run rampant.  Examples of such innovations from MIT’s Magazine of Innovation Technology Review (February, 2003) include injectable tissue engineering, nano solar cells, wireless sensor networks, mechantronics, grid computing, molecular imaging, nanoimprint lithography, software assurance, glycomics, and quantum cryptography.  A search on these terms will enable you to get a summary of each of these technologies.  You can also check out MIT’s website on new technologies:

As a company, you would need to review the viability of an emerging technology that might provide strategic advantage.  A simple way to review such technologies would include a review of their intended use, costs/drawbacks, practical applications, competing technologies, widespread use (diffusion), and impact on the organization or, for this course, impact on the web professional.  Sample reviews of some emerging technologies follow: http://web.media.mit.edu

Enhanced Reality

Intended Use:

Enhanced reality combines hardware and software to enable the user to use technology to increase the accuracy, ease, and ability of executing every day functions.  Examples range from holding one's daily schedule in a user's watch or other personal trigger object that would remind the user about meetings to assisting in an operation by projecting the image of previously conducted surgery to act as a step by step procedural guide. 

Costs/Drawbacks:

The costs associated with enhanced reality can be high.  Software and hardware range from relatively inexpensive (such as using a PDA) to high cost hardware and software development.  Therefore, one must decide “at what cost” do we develop and use enhanced reality.  For example, if enhanced reality in an operating room environment increases the survival rate of the patient significantly over traditional operating room technology, then one can argue that the cost is worth it.  However uncomfortable, decisions will need to be made regarding heath care costs and insurance reimbursement for enhanced reality.  It may come down to the “haves” and “have nots” in terms of access to expensive health care which integrates enhanced reality.  In fact, overall, the digital divide may be replaced by the technology affordable divide.  You won’t need to understand how to use the technology, but you may need to be able to pay for it.

Regardless of costs issues, there are additional drawbacks associated with enhanced reality.  For example, one may question whether enhanced reality actually deepens our sensory contact with reality or in fact reduces it.  Thus, we may end up questioning the actual definition of reality.  Some may argue that enhanced technology actually warps one’s perception of reality; however, I believe that enhanced reality will allow us to understand and appreciate a deepened sensory and perceptual experience that will be tantamount to a richer and more vivid understanding of the “real” world.

Finally, less philosophical, more practical drawbacks include the uncomfortable addition of hardware and software in our daily lives.  So, it may mean wearing a hard hat with audio and video equipment attached to engage our enhanced reality.  It will also, I argue, mean that we have less “down” time and more stress associated with an increased desire to achieve the productivity enabled by enhanced reality.

Practical Applications

A typical user of enhanced reality would encounter uses like the following:

-         A teacher or storyteller could read a picture book and associate each picture with the text that is triggered by audio clips assigned to each page.  Then, a pre-reader can look at the picture and listen to the audio clips.

-         A factory worker or ordinary consumer can view real time instructions for assembly which are presented based upon completion of each step.

-         A visually challenged person could benefit by a verbal description of objects in his/her field of view.

-         An important client conversation can be recorded and placed on the person’s business card.

-         The visual appearance of an object can be augmented by relevant audio and video.  Examples of this are already in place in many museums.

Additional examples are endless…

Competing Technologies

Virtual reality can be perceived as competition to enhanced reality.  Many people may prefer to “doctor-up” the real world with fantasy.  In other words, they might prefer to spend time in a virtual world rather than the “real world”.  In a virtual world one can integrate reality with fantasy for the amusement of the user.  We have all experienced this desire when we choose to go to the movies and view fiction over a documentary.  Game playing also uses a lot of virtual reality.  These are forms of entertainment.  However, where does one draw the line and where are you willing to spend your discretionary income?  Personally, while I often choose fiction over nonfiction, I prefer to purchase technology that will enhance my everyday functioning rather than spend money to enhance my ability to engage in “virtual reality”.  For example, one of my recent purchases was a digital camera to improve my memory of vacations, students, and special events.  Reality that is not enhanced or “every-day” reality also competes with enhance reality.

Widespread Use of Technology

Whether or not one considers enhanced reality to be widespread depends on how one defines enhanced reality.  For example, eye glasses can actually be considered a form of enhanced reality.  If one narrows the definition to include the use of technologically advanced hardware and software to increase the accuracy, ease, and ability of executing every day functions, then enhanced reality is not as widespread.  However, we still see enhanced reality available to the average person in museums, hospitals and doctors’ offices, as well as product production in technologically advanced factories.  With greater diffusion of technological advances, enhanced reality will become even more widespread in the future.

Impact on the Web Professional

Web professionals will need to stay informed about the impact of these advanced technologies and may need to integrate these technologies into web development.  For example, they may need to allow access to these technologies from the web by making audio/video streaming downloadable to a user’s enhanced reality hardware/software through the web.  New devices will replace the traditional desktop and laptop computers and peripherals which we use to with web technologies. 

 

Accessibility Issues

Visually and/or hearing impaired individuals may be able to benefit from enhanced reality to the extent that it will improve their existing senses.  Low income or technophobes may not experience the benefits of enhanced reality.

Electronic Paper

Intended Use:

Electronic paper (e-paper) is an emerging technology that combines the convenience of paper or a paper-like medium with digital information such that the paper becomes reusable.  The intended use is to enable the user to reuse the paper in order to read books, magazines, and newspapers.  It is thought that some day this medium will actually replace traditional books, magazines, and newspapers.  Although electronic paper has actually been around for a long time, only recent technological advances have made the concept viable.  The technology is currently being used commercially for advertising signs, mainly on in-store displays.

Costs/Drawbacks:

E-paper is made from relatively inexpensive materials, and the manufacturing process is cheaper than that of LCDs.  However, it is more expensive than paper.  One can recover the cost over a period of time that is contingent on the level of use.  However, if you want to have multiple books open at one time, you would need multiple e-paper binders. 

If e-paper is produced in mass quantities, the costs will become much lower.  Eventually several hundred sheets can be wired together into a book that will cost less than a computer screen.  The cost savings lies in the fact that each sheet can be erased and filled with more text repeatedly.  A single book composed of e-paper could be used and reused more than a million times. 

There is low power consumption required by e-paper.  Once the information is downloaded or “printed” to the e-paper, there is no additional need for a power supply until one wants to change or update the pages because there is a low need for power to operate the e-paper.  Furthermore, the e-paper can be updated via wireless technology.  Future improvements in the technology will render the paper flexible enough to be rolled up. 

Risks or drawbacks associated with e-paper include a lack of  manufacturers willing to invest in such products until the marketplace has been tested.  And, it is uncertain if the market interest will be widespread.  There are still problems associated with the lifespan of current e-paper such that displays currently last between 10,000 and 30,000 hours.  Although there is a low power requirement, the batteries associated with e-paper currently present a problem with weight and flexibility. 

Practical Applications:

In-store advertising and information signs currently make use of e-paper technology.  In the future, books, magazines, and newspapers may adopt the technology.  Although you can presently use your computer to access reading materials, the convenience of paper that you can take with you anywhere may be what the consumer wants.  Thus, practical applications of e-paper may have a shot in the market place.

Competing Technologies:

Competing technologies range from news print, books, and magazines to LCD displays and PDA devices.  So, either one will use traditional sources for reading material or one will use existing alternative technological solutions for  viewing printed material.  E-books are already available on-line.  People can read them or down load them to their PDA or pocket pc.

Diffusion of Technology:

Electronic paper is not very widespread.  Current usage is primarily for in-store signs.  There is still hope that the technology will become widespread with recent technological advances in the development of e-paper.  However, people are already downloading e-books to pocket pc’s.  Perhaps people who are likely to use e-paper are already using existing technology.  Others may not want to replace traditional books, magazines, and newspapers. 

Affect on the Web Professional:

The web professional will have an increased need to recognize that information will be downloaded to varying devices including e-paper.  The formatting for e-paper may be very different from that associated with different web browsers or PDA’s.  Thus, the web professional will need to become increasingly flexible with adjusting to new peripherals and their specific formatting requirements.

Accessibility Issues

Blind or visually impaired individuals will not be able to use e-paper because there is no plan to introduce a Braille version of the new technology.  Initially, cost may be an issue for people interested in using e-paper.  Over time, cost should decrease to enable greater accessibility.

Automatic Code Generation from Pictorial Design Patterns

Intended Use:

Design patterns increase the level of abstraction for which people can design and communicate the design of software.  A design pattern describes a solution to a recurring software problem in a systematic way in an attempt to capture expertise in building software.  This is intended as a way to decrease development time in code generation for people already trained in code generation.  However, it is also possible for people to successfully use the software without programming experience.  Code generation by simply pointing and clicking on pictorial representations of your design pattern will mean increased speed and ease in code development for all.

Costs/Drawbacks:

The costs of automatic code generation from pictorial design patterns consist of the purchase price of the software and start-up time to learn automatic code generation.  Drawbacks associated with this new technology include the development of inefficient code.  A good programmer would probably be able to do a better job of writing the code.  However, a mediocre coder may not do as good a job.

Often the code generated automatically is not user friendly.  It may be difficult for programmers to understand the code well enough to go in and make their own changes.  Thus, it may be that changes are more difficult to make compared to programmer generated code development.. 

Practical Applications:

Practical applications for automatic code generation are endless.  They include all applications relevant to human code generation.  Automatic code generation from pictorial design patterns will eventually be available to write almost any programming code.  Thus, practically speaking, programmers may not be in as great a demand because of this new technology.  However, the jury is still out on this one!  Others argue that one should be skilled in the underlying program generation in order to successfully use automatic code generation.  I would argue that it may be more important to be skilled in the practical application rather than the code generation.  For example, in my opinion, it is more important for a scientist (engineer or mathematician) to understand a project application when using MathWorks’ Simulink, than to understand how to write the code that is generated by the software.  Specific practical applications for Simulink include: system-level design of digital signal processing and communication systems, from algorithm development to simulation, rapid prototyping, and verification.  Digital signal processing and communications design has traditionally been tackled by two groups, a developer designing advanced algorithms and an engineer implementing the design in hardware and software.  Simulink allows you to develop, test, and verify designs, i.e., the whole project from concept to testing to coding.  You can design and test satellite communication’s links or test and design aircraft prototypes.

Using Vision JADE, another pictorial design pattern software product, officials at Hilton Hotels Corp., in Beverly Hills, Calif., claim better than ten-fold gains in application time to market (six weeks for full development vs. three months to prototype 10% of the system using conventional tools.  Applications for automatic code generation from pictorial design patterns appear endless.

Competing Technologies:

Competing technologies range from hand coding traditional programs to using object-oriented software that does not include automatic implementation of  pictorial design patterns, e.g., C.  Within the competing technology, there is increased competition for code generation software although there is room for coexistence of many tools since they often focus on a particular software application area (e.g., engineering or manufacturing).  One example within the engineering realm is SimuLink by MathWorks.  Another example is Vision JADE, a CASE tool for Java-based Web development. It allows developers to design applications as models and automatically generate Java code. 

Diffusion of Technology:

http://www.mathworks.com/products/

This technology is rapidly diffusing as software is rapidly being written for automatic code generation for numerous disciplines.  Research is available regarding how to develop such code and is freely available off the internet (e.g., Automatic Code Generation from Design Patterns by Budinsky, Finnie, and Vlissides). 

Because application possibilities are endless, this technology will be diffused like wild fire!

Affect on the Web Professional:

Life will get more complicated as automatic code generation from pictorial design patterns diffuses throughout web applications.  For example, with a few clicks, it is now possible to develop and customize a portal system complete with database applications.  This will mean that more people will demand greater functionality in their web sites.  It may also mean that more people will develop their own applications without consulting a web professional. 

Accessibility Issues:

Visually impaired individuals are shut out of this technology at present.  Cost impediments may also exist for some.
